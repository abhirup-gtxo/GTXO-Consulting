CASE STUDY

From High CPL to 5.5x ROAS in 13 Weeks

Scaling a D2C Astrology Product on Meta Ads, 3x revenue growth in 4 months

D2C Paid

THE SITUATION



Good product, stuck campaigns


AstroUrjaa is a D2C astrology store offering astrology-led products for urban consumers who actively believe in astrology and are willing to spend on it. Revenue comes through online purchases — so everything we optimised around was the cost of getting someone to make their first purchase.


Their audience is urban youth, 18–35, who genuinely believe in astrology and are willing to pay for astrology-based products. High intent, decent paying capacity — the right people were out there. The problem was the campaigns weren't reaching them efficiently.


They'd been running Meta ads in-house for 3–4 months. CPL was stuck at ₹138 and not moving. Volumes were low. Campaigns were broad, budget was spread flat, and there was no real way to tell what was working.

The product-market fit was solid. The issue was entirely in how the campaigns were structured.


WHAT WE CHANGED


Audiences first


The biggest shift was in how we built audiences. Instead of using interest-based targeting — which is what most in-house teams default to — we went back to the platform's own data.


We created lookalike audiences from customers who had already purchased ₹2,000 or more, and separately from customers who had placed five or more orders. These aren't just website visitors or casual browsers — they're people who've bought, repeatedly. Lookalikes built from that source are a completely different quality than lookalikes built from traffic or add-to-cart events.


As the campaign matured and we had more data, we added a third high-value lookalike targeting the top-spending customer cohort. Each new audience layer sharpened the targeting further.


Alongside acquisition, we ran remarketing campaigns in parallel — because a lot of high-intent shoppers don't purchase on the first touch, and without remarketing you're just losing them.


HOW WE FOUND WHAT WORKED


Testing with a structure, not a gut feel


One of the clearest gaps in the in-house setup was that creatives were being changed without a real testing framework. So you'd swap out an ad, see numbers move, and have no idea if it was the creative, the audience, the timing, or just noise.

We ran structured parallel tests across four angles at the same time:


• Age-specific messaging — a 22-year-old anxious about a relationship needs a completely different hook than a 32-year-old worried about a career plateau

• Interest-based angles — content tailored to the specific astrology categories and product types users were already engaging with

• Problem-first framing — ads that led with the actual anxieties that push someone to buy astrology-led products, not just generic astrology content

• Budget thresholds — testing how different spend levels affected efficiency across campaign types

Testing in parallel meant we could isolate variables properly. Within a few weeks we had a clear read on what was resonating — and we scaled what worked instead of spending time on what didn't.


04 — BUDGET AS A VARIABLE, NOT A SETTING

Where and when you spend matters as much as how much



Early on, the data showed something interesting: Fridays consistently outperformed every other day for first-purchase conversions on this platform. It's a category-specific behaviour — people are more likely to spend on personal, spiritual, and lifestyle products going into the weekend.



So we weighted budget toward Fridays instead of spreading it evenly across the week. That alone improved efficiency without changing anything else.


Beyond day-of-week, we split budget between new customer acquisition and remarketing based on audience freshness — how recently someone had been exposed to the brand or visited the store. Pouring spend into an audience that's already seen your ads multiple times is wasteful. We tracked this and adjusted weekly, not monthly.


Budget reallocation happened every week based on what the numbers were showing. If a campaign was underperforming, spend moved. Nothing sat idle.


One thing worth calling out: cost per first purchase came down while we were simultaneously scaling spend. Usually when you push more budget in, efficiency drops — you start reaching less relevant audiences. That didn't happen here, because the audience architecture was built to hold.
